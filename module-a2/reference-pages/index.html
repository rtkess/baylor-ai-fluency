When in doubt, ask: if AI gets this wrong and I don't catch it, what is the cost? The higher and more irreversible that cost, the more the task belongs with you.

The quality of what AI gives you is proportional to the quality of how you ask. CARE is a four-part structure for prompts that produce usable, discipline-appropriate output on the first pass. Include all four whenever the output matters.

C

Context

Set the scene. Tell AI who you are, what course you teach, your students' level, and relevant background.

Example"I teach FIN 3310, a junior-level Corporate Finance course at Baylor's Hankamer School of Business. Students just completed a session on capital structure theory."

A

Action

State exactly what you want AI to do — the task, the quantity, the format, and the cognitive level you're targeting.

Example"Generate four scenario-based quiz questions that require students to apply capital structure trade-offs to a realistic firm decision — not recall definitions."

R

Role

Tell AI what expertise to adopt. This shapes the tone, depth, and disciplinary framing of the response.

Example"Act as an experienced corporate finance educator familiar with Modigliani-Miller propositions and Hankamer's expectations for analytical rigor."

E

Example

Show AI what good looks like — a sample, a model, or the depth and style you expect. The most-skipped component, and the one that most improves output.

Example"Target Bloom's level: Application and Analysis. Format each item as a 2–3 sentence firm scenario followed by one focused analytical question."

A prompt missing Context produces generic output. A prompt missing Example produces output at the wrong depth. When the result matters, use all four.

AI does not know when it is wrong, and a confident tone is not a reliability signal. Before any AI-generated content reaches your students, run it through these five checks. Four protect quality; the +1 protects access.

1

Accurate?

Is every fact, figure, citation, and claim correct? AI can state false information — invented quotes, fabricated sources — with complete confidence.

Watch for: misattributed quotes, plausible but wrong statistics, citations that don't exist.

2

Mine?

Does this sound like you, and does it fit your students? AI output is generic by default. Revise until the voice and emphasis reflect your course.

Watch for: tone that doesn't match your style, examples that don't fit your students.

3

Aligned?

Does this actually serve your learning objectives? Polished output that drifts from your goals is still off-target.

Watch for: content that's impressive but tangential to your stated outcomes.

4

Safe?

Did you protect privacy and avoid harm? Never put student names, grades, or identifiable information into a general-purpose AI tool — that's a FERPA concern.

Watch for: FERPA-protected data in prompts, biased framing, content that could exclude students.

The +1 — accessibility check
5

Accessible?

Can every student use this? Ask AI directly: "What accessibility barriers exist here, and what specific revisions would reduce them?" Addressing access before a student has to request an accommodation is the professional standard.

Watch for: images without alt text, dense unstructured text, color used as the only way to convey meaning.

Run all five every time. The check that takes two minutes is what makes you the author of AI-assisted work — not the editor of someone else's mistakes.
